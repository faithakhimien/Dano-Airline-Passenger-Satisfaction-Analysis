## Introduction:

Dano Airlines, a UK-based airline headquartered in London, recently recorded a passenger satisfaction rate below 50% for the first time. This decline signals growing dissatisfaction among customers and presents a major business concern, as customer satisfaction directly influences loyalty, retention, and profitability.

This project analyzes over 120,000 passenger survey records to identify the major factors contributing to dissatisfaction and provide data-driven recommendations for improving customer satisfaction.


## Key Metrics: 

1. What is the Overall Satisfaction Rate
2. What is the Lowest Rated Service
3. Lowest Satisfaction Segment
4. Most Dissatisfied Travel Type
5. Age Group Satisfaction Patterns
6. Returning & First-time Customers Comparison

### Analysis:

### Overall Satisfaction Rate
At 43.4%, Dano’s satisfaction rate has fallen below 50% for the first time in the airline’s history. That number alone is concerning, but what sits behind it is even more telling. The overall figure is being kept afloat almost entirely by Business class passengers at 69.4% and returning customers at 66.6%. Strip those groups away and the picture deteriorates quickly. 
Economy passengers, personal travelers, and younger age groups are all falling well below the average, and their dissatisfaction is what is dragging the overall rate down.

### Lowest Rated Service
In-flight Wifi and Online Booking scored 2.73 and 2.76 out of 5 respectively, making them the two weakest services across the entire survey. Passengers today expect to stay connected whether they are working mid-flight or streaming entertainment, and Dano is not meeting that expectation. These scores are not just low in isolation. They signal a digital experience that is actively working against satisfaction across every passenger segment.

### Lowest Satisfaction Segment
Economy class sits at just 18.8% satisfaction, a striking gap when placed next to Business class at 69.4% and even Economy Plus at 24.6%. What makes this particularly significant is that Economy carries the largest share of passengers. A satisfaction rate that low, spread across that volume of travelers, has a disproportionate pull on the overall score. Improving Economy class experience is not just a service issue. It is the single biggest lever available for moving the overall satisfaction rate.

### Most Dissatisfied Travel Type
Only 10.1% of personal travelers are satisfied with their Dano experience, compared to 58.4% of business travelers. That is not a small gap. It reflects a service model that has been built almost entirely around the needs of corporate passengers, frequent flyers who travel on predictable schedules with predictable needs. Leisure travelers bring different expectations around flexibility, comfort, and value, and right now those expectations are going largely unmet.

### Age Group Satisfaction
The 46 to 60 age group records the highest satisfaction at 57.4%, which aligns closely with Dano’s core business traveler profile. Outside of that bracket, the numbers drop sharply. Under-18 passengers sit at 16.7%, the 18 to 25 group at 34.7%, and seniors aged 60 and above at 20.8%. Younger and older passengers are clearly experiencing a version of Dano that was not designed with them in mind, and the scores reflect that disconnect consistently.

### Returning and First-Time Customers
Returning customers account for 66.6% of all passengers surveyed, which looks like a healthy loyalty base on the surface. But with an overall satisfaction rate of 43.4%, a significant number of those returning customers are coming back despite being dissatisfied, not because they are happy. That kind of passive loyalty does not hold indefinitely. If the experience does not improve, Dano risks losing the very customer base that has been propping up its numbers, and replacing a loyal customer costs far more than retaining one.

## Conclusion and Recommendations:

Dano’s satisfaction problem is not spread evenly. It lives in specific segments that have clearly been deprioritized. Economy passengers, personal travelers, first-time customers, and passengers on either end of the age spectrum are all experiencing an airline that was not built with them in mind. Business class and returning corporate travelers are the only reason the overall rate has not fallen further. The data is consistent: Dano has optimized for one type of passenger and left everyone else behind. Closing that gap is not just a service improvement project. It is a business priority.

The digital experience is where Dano needs to act first. Wifi and Online Booking sitting below 2.8 out of 5 creates daily friction for every passenger regardless of class or travel type. Upgrading connectivity and rebuilding the booking experience should be the first investment on the table. 

Economy class needs to follow closely. An 18.8% satisfaction rate across the largest passenger group cannot be fixed with small adjustments. Service consistency, cleanliness, and entertainment access need to close the gap with Economy Plus in a meaningful way.

Personal travelers at 89.9% dissatisfaction need their own strategy, not modifications to a corporate model. Flexible booking, family-friendly options, and leisure-oriented perks would signal that Dano sees this segment as a priority. 

Average delays of around 15 minutes on both departure and arrival also need to come down, as punctuality is a baseline expectation across all passenger types. 

Finally, a loyalty program tailored to the Under-18 and 60-plus groups would be a practical starting point for turning Dano’s most overlooked demographics into a genuine growth opportunity.
